## Immigration Violations: The Biggest Gap

### Why This Matters:

**Immigration violations are included in "procedural grounds" (16% denial rate) but specific statistics for K-1 are not publicly available.**

Common immigration violations that would disqualify K-1 applicants:

1. **Previous deportation/removal:** Automatic bar (requires waiver, rarely granted for K-1)
2. **Unlawful presence 180-364 days:** 3-year bar
3. **Unlawful presence 365+ days:** 10-year bar
4. **Fraud/misrepresentation:** Permanent bar (requires waiver)
5. **Entered without inspection:** Complex, often requires waiver
6. **Prior visa denial for material reason:** Not automatic bar but makes approval harder

### Self-Selection Question:

**You asked:** "If I had been deported before, wouldn't I Google this first?"

**Answer:** Yes and no.

**Self-selection DOES occur for:**
- Obvious bars (recent deportation, major criminal conviction)
- People who already consulted lawyers
- Educated applicants who research thoroughly

**Self-selection DOES NOT occur for:**
- **Minor overstays** (many people don't realize a 2-week overstay 5 years ago matters)
- **Visa denials that seem unrelated** ("That was a tourist visa denial, this is different!")
- **Complex situations** (entered legally, overstayed, left, now applying - is there a bar?)
- **Expunged/dismissed cases** (criminal or immigration - people think it "doesn't count")
- **Misrepresentation they don't realize was misrepresentation** (gave wrong address on tourist visa)

**Practical Example:**
- User had a B-2 tourist visa denied in 2019 for "insufficient ties to home country"
- They think: "That was just a tourist visa, K-1 is different because I have a U.S. citizen sponsor!"
- Reality: Prior denial creates scrutiny, may require explanation, could affect approval
- **Your platform wouldn't catch this** until after payment

## Conclusion

Your instinct about self-selection is **partially correct** - people with obvious bars (recent deportation, major criminal conviction) often do Google first and self-select out.

**However**, the data shows:
- 16% of immigrant visa denials are "procedural grounds" (includes immigration violations)
- Even if only 3-5% of K-1 applicants have immigration violations (conservative estimate), that's significant
- **Without screening for this, ~3-5 out of 100 paying customers would be ineligible**

**Adding immigration violation screening would likely get you to 85-90% coverage**, which is close to your 95% goal and probably good enough given data limitations and self-selection effects.
